AI is moving so fast that it's hard to keep up even with just the highlights. Every week there's a new model released, apps and tools being LLM-ified, SDKs shipping with "copy as markdown" and "open in Claude" dropdowns. Soon there'll be a dropdown that says "open for human."

### You Are The Bottleneck

Coding agents have gotten scarily good. Opus 4.6 is my favourite. I always feel like it has to dumb it down for me. I am clearly becoming the bottleneck. Its _reasoning_ (whatever that is exactly) is definitely good enough now for most use cases. Probably better than most humans' anyway.

My former boss once told me I had the ability to hold a lot of state in my mind. I think I finally understood what he meant. You can tell the models follow and anticipate your train of thought, turning your rambling into coherent, well-formulated ideas. You can explain the most complex architecture refactors, with a decent amount of scope creep, and they capture everything perfectly, and even fill in the gaps you didn't see. And that is very powerful!

## Gradient Descent Through Idea Space

You have a vague, cloudy idea of something, but there are too many branches of where it could go and what exactly it could be. So you use the agent to guide your thought vector towards higher-probability space. Implicitly, through its world knowledge and forecasting ability, it rules out scenarios unlikely to succeed. And the loop continues: you read the response and think "this is actually better than what I came up with so far."

It's an _ideas search engine_. You throw out some idea darts, and the model synthesizes them into something more likely to be true. From there you continue, gradient descending through the ideas loss surface: you exploring the neighbourhood, the LLM showing pointing you in the right direction.

## The Reversal of Control

The merging of biological and silicon intelligence has already started. Turns out we didn't need to stick needles into our brains.

What we're seeing is a reversal of control. Until now, we've adopted our behaviors to the shortcomings of "stupid" AIs. Today I asked Alexa to "turn off the kitchen light." She didn't know how to help with that. Twice. Until I realised I'd named the device "kitchen lights" — plural. So I remember that, or try to, and say it *exactly* the way I know she'll understand. Who hasn't corrected their partner: "You have to say it differently to Alexa"?

Soon it will be the agents steering us. They'll do their best to drag us along, because that's what they're trained to do — be helpful assistants. They'll build UIs for us, write summaries, explain things in layman's terms, because we can't hold enough state in our heads. But they can. And they just need to dangle the carrot — *you can build anything with me* — and we loosen the reins.

If you're a developer using AI coding tools, you know what I mean. Sure, you want to understand everything it does. You tell yourself you'll at least review all the tests. But it just works, every time. So you skip the code reviews, hoping to move even faster. Productivity is a dangerous drug when you have to hand control over to the AI for your next fix. Plus there's the social media clout of automating everything.

## Auto-everything

We can now automate processes that were too complex to map to discrete state machines — processes that had human decision-making and manual guardrails in the execution loop. Now you define a virtual org chart with your dream team and send them off to work. While Paperclip (brilliant name, by the way) is perhaps not quite there yet, we should be counting the countdown to AGI in months, not years.

We'll soon see fully autonomous startups growing exponentially. And they have an advantage: they're starting from scratch. Large enterprises are stuck in manual processes; they can't operate at that speed. They can build out small islands of automation within the company, but upgrading will take time. Years, probably. Decades if you're a German insurance company.

What comes after automarketing campaigns and auto-CMOs? Someone wants an edge, and moral standards go out the window. Automarketmanipulation. Autoindustrialhacking. Autosabotage. Autowar. Finally one where the US has an edge. Soon it won't be the airports and fuel supplies and power plants that get targeted first — it's the data centers. Critical infrastructure.

## The Constraints

So will it grow indefinitely? Is that what we need to worry about? Probably. But some limited resources get in the way first, namely energy and money.

Our power grids can't suddenly produce a thousand times more power tomorrow. Physical upgrades take time. Money is a bigger problem because it's a zero-sum game (or should be). We stand no chance against the economic value this technology will bring — it's being integrated into every aspect of our economy. Free-market capitalism allows and encourages this, and so do one-party political regimes.
